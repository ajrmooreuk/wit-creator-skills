# Script — "You Don't Need a CTO — You Need This"

**Target length:** Medium (8-10 min)
**Title variant:** A (Contrarian)

---

## [0:00] HOOK

"Every time a growing business hits a tech problem, someone says 'you need to hire a CTO.' And look — maybe you do eventually. But right now? You don't need a £120K salary. You need a single page. And by the end of this video, you'll have it."

---

## [0:18] PROBLEM

"Here's what I see over and over. A company hits 50, 80, 100 people. Suddenly the spreadsheets don't work, the tools don't talk to each other, and the ops manager — usually a woman, by the way — is holding the entire tech strategy together in her head.

So someone says: 'We need a CTO.' And then one of two things happens. Either you spend six months hiring someone who costs more than your marketing budget. Or you hire a consultant who gives you 200 slides and leaves.

Neither of those solves the actual problem. The actual problem is: nobody has written down what your tech does, what it should do, and what order to fix it in. That's not a CTO problem. That's a one-page problem."

[B-ROLL: Montage — chaotic spreadsheets, Slack messages, someone on a Zoom call looking overwhelmed]

---

## [1:30] SOLUTION

"Here's the one-page tech roadmap I use. Three sections. Takes about an hour to fill in. Changes everything.

**Step 1: What We Have (5 minutes)**

List every tool, platform, and system your business uses right now. I don't mean the official list — I mean the real one. The Airtable someone set up. The shared Google Drive. The WhatsApp group that's actually running operations.

Just list them. Don't judge. The goal is to see reality."

[SCREEN: Show a real example — a simple table: Tool | What it does | Who uses it | Pain level 1-5]

"**Step 2: What Hurts (20 minutes)**

Now go through that list and mark the pain points. Where do things break? Where do people waste time? Where does data get stuck?

I use a 1-to-5 pain scale. 1 is 'it's annoying.' 5 is 'this will cost us a client or a staff member if we don't fix it.'

[EMPHASIS] Anything that's a 4 or 5? That's your roadmap. Everything else waits."

[SCREEN: Show the same table with pain scores highlighted — the 4s and 5s jump out]

"**Step 3: What We Fix (30 minutes)**

Take your 4s and 5s. For each one, write one sentence: what does 'fixed' look like?

Not 'implement an enterprise integration platform.' Just: 'Client data lives in one place and the sales team can see it without asking me.'

Then put them in order. The rule is: [EMPHASIS] fix the thing that unblocks the most other things first. Usually that's data — get your data right and half the other problems shrink.

That's your roadmap. One page. Three columns. You can present it to the board in five minutes."

[SCREEN: Show the completed one-page roadmap — What We Have | What Hurts | What We Fix Next]

---

## [7:00] PROOF

"I've used this exact format across five different businesses — from an antiques intelligence startup to a workplace methodology company to a care sector platform. Different industries, same pattern. Every single one had the same reaction: 'Why didn't we do this two years ago?'

One of them — a 60-person company — used this one page to justify their entire tech budget for the year. No consultant. No CTO. One page and an hour of honest work."
